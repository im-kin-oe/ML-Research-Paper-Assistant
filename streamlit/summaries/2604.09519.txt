Title: Toward World Models for Epidemiology

**TL;DR:** This paper introduces a conceptual framework for epidemiological world models, which learn to simulate and predict the spread of diseases, taking into account latent dynamics, noisy observations, and adaptive human behavior. The framework aims to support decision-making and policy analysis in computational epidemiology.

**Problem:** The paper addresses the limitations of current epidemiological models, which often assume fixed dynamics, fixed behavior, and fixed observation processes, and fail to account for the complexities of real-world disease spread, such as strategic misreporting, systematic delays, and counterfactual intervention analysis.

**Method:** The authors propose a world model framework that formulates epidemics as controlled, partially observed dynamical systems, which includes a latent state representation, transition dynamics, observation functions, and interventions. The framework is designed to support robust decision-making under partial observability, endogenous measurement, and model misspecification.

**Results:** The paper presents three case studies that demonstrate the effectiveness of the world model framework in supporting policy-relevant reasoning, including strategic misreporting in behavioral surveillance, systematic delays in time-lagged signals, and counterfactual intervention analysis.

**Why it matters:** This research matters because it has the potential to improve the accuracy and reliability of epidemiological models, which are critical for informing public health policy and decision-making. By accounting for the complexities of real-world disease spread, the world model framework can help policymakers make more informed decisions and develop more effective interventions.

**3 things to remember:**
- Epidemiological world models can learn to simulate and predict the spread of diseases, taking into account latent dynamics, noisy observations, and adaptive human behavior.
- The world model framework formulates epidemics as controlled, partially observed dynamical systems, which includes a latent state representation, transition dynamics, observation functions, and interventions.
- The framework has the potential to support robust decision-making under partial observability, endogenous measurement, and model misspecification, and can be applied to a range of policy-relevant scenarios, including strategic misreporting, systematic delays, and counterfactual intervention analysis.
